# Audience — DTC (Direct-to-Consumer)

Floki's direct-to-consumer customer. Two distinct segments within the DTC market.

See [04-AUDIENCE-TRADE.md](./04-AUDIENCE-TRADE.md) for B2B / trade audience profiles.
See [01-BRAND-THESIS.md](./01-BRAND-THESIS.md) for who these profiles are built against.

---

## Segment 1 — The Considerate Buyer

The primary DTC customer is not an impulse buyer. They are a curator.

They buy less and buy better. They research. They look at something for two weeks before purchasing. They follow interior accounts and style accounts but would not describe themselves as "into home decor" — they just care about how things feel.

**Profile**
- Age: 28–42
- Location: Urban markets — NYC, LA, Chicago, Atlanta, DC, Miami, and near-urban orbits
- Income: $65K–$150K household
- Mindset: Quality over quantity. Anti-clutter. Treats a purchase as an investment.
- Shops at: Everlane, Madewell, West Elm, Muji — occasionally Etsy for the right piece
- Follows: Interior accounts, fashion basics, capsule wardrobe content, African lifestyle creators
- Decision tempo: Slow. Price is not the primary objection — worth is.

**What Motivates Purchase**
1. Aesthetic fit. Does this belong in my space?
2. Proof of quality. Reviews, imagery, material specifics. It has to look as considered as their eye is.
3. The hospitality signal. Seeing Floki in a hotel, or reading that hotels use it, closes the trust gap immediately.
4. Cultural resonance (pillow buyers — see Segment 2).

**What Doesn't Work on Them**
- Discount urgency ("Sale ends tonight!")
- Hollow claims ("Elevate your space!")
- Over-produced imagery that looks artificial
- Aggressive retargeting before they are ready

**Copy approach:** Short, factual, confident. Let the product speak. See [05-BRAND-VOICE.md](./05-BRAND-VOICE.md).

## Segment 2 — The Diaspora Consumer

African print pillow buyers with specific motivations beyond the general considerate buyer profile.

**Profile**
- African or Caribbean diaspora, or deeply connected to African design culture
- Age: 26–45
- Has spent years looking for home goods that reflect their aesthetic and heritage — without settling for quality that doesn't match the significance of the culture
- Wants design that is culturally grounded and contemporary premium — not "ethnic store"
- Has been frustrated that "elevated home decor" defaults to Western neutral
- Follows: Cee Cee's Closet, Hanifa, Fe Noel, Fe Noel, African contemporary interior and lifestyle accounts

**What Motivates Purchase**
1. Recognition. "This was made for me."
2. Quality parity. The product is as well-made as the cultural reference is meaningful.
3. Pride. Owning something that represents African design at a premium standard.

**What Doesn't Work**
- Tokenizing language ("Celebrate your heritage!")
- Treating the cultural element as a feature rather than the foundation
- Poor product quality that undermines the cultural representation

**Copy approach:** Warm but not patronizing. Speak to the design and craft roots. Don't over-explain. They know. The copy reflects, it does not educate.
