# Visual Identity — Floki Marketing

The marketing-side brief for all visual assets across Floki. Defines what is needed, why, and the visual system that governs everything. Design execution lives in `Websites/design/`.

Marketing owns the **what**. Design owns the **how**.

---

## Visual System Overview

Floki's visual identity operates within three distinct pillars. Every asset — photography, video, graphic, campaign material — maps to one. Consistency within the system is what builds visual brand recognition over time.

---

## The Three Visual Pillars

### 1. Editorial Minimal

Clean. Considered. Premium. White space does the work. Product is the hero. No clutter.

- **Used for:** Product pages, hero imagery, collection banners, paid ads
- **Mood:** Quiet confidence. The kind of room that does not try to impress you.
- **Color:** Brand palette primaries — white, cream, off-black. Product color as the accent.
- **Reference direction:** Studio product photography, high-fashion lookbooks, Kinfolk-style home editorial, Japanese craft product photography

### 2. Lifestyle Context

Product in its natural environment. Real spaces, real moments. Aspirational but lived-in.

- **Used for:** Our Story page, social content, email campaigns, STR/hotel lifestyle imagery
- **Mood:** Aspirational but attainable. A space that has been lived in by someone with taste.
- **Color:** Warm naturals — linen, wood, stone — with Floki product color as the accent point.
- **Reference direction:** Apartment Therapy editorial, minimal wardrobe content, African home decor editorial, boutique hotel room photography

### 3. Cultural Richness

Bold color, pattern, and texture. For the African print collection. Not decoration — design language.

- **Used for:** Pillow collection, cultural brand storytelling moments, campaign hero images
- **Mood:** Proud, vivid, modern. Not costume. Culture expressed at a contemporary premium level.
- **Color:** Deep saturated tones from the textile source — the print provides the palette, the background supports it.
- **Reference direction:** Contemporary African design editorial, Adire and Ankara textile photography, Afrofuturist visual culture, Lagos contemporary interiors

---

## Visual Rules

These apply across all three pillars.

**Breathing room.** No cluttered compositions. Every image needs space for the eye to rest.

**Product integrity.** No distorted perspectives. No obvious AI artifacts. The product should look exactly like the product.

**Color discipline.** All imagery should be compatible with the brand palette (see `Websites/design/02-STYLE-GUIDE.md`). No warm/cool whiplash within a single feed or campaign.

**Lighting consistency.** Natural or soft studio light. No harsh flash. No over-filtered treatment.

**The hotel test.** Before approving any visual: would a design-forward boutique hotel use this as a reference image for their wardrobe brief? If yes, it belongs.

### Trade / Hotel Audience

Any visual showing Floki in a hotel wardrobe or property context should read as aspirational and professional simultaneously. It is not a lifestyle shot — it is a proof shot. Hotel hallway wardrobe, open door, Floki hangers visible. Clean, real, credible.

## Visual Brief Process

1. **Marketing identifies the need** — campaign, page section, social content slot
2. **Marketing writes a brief in Active Briefs below** — pillar, product, placement, mood, deadline
3. **Design picks it up** — runs through reference system and AI workflow (`Websites/design/03-AI-DESIGN-WORKFLOW.md`)
4. **Output returns to marketing** — reviewed against campaign intent and the visual rules above
5. **Approved assets go to dev** — implementation handoff

## Asset Review Checklist

Before approving any visual asset:

- [ ] Maps to one of the three visual pillars
- [ ] Compatible with brand palette (see `Websites/design/02-STYLE-GUIDE.md`)
- [ ] No AI artifacts or distortions
- [ ] Breathing room in composition — not cluttered
- [ ] Would pass the hotel test
- [ ] File named using convention in the Style Guide
